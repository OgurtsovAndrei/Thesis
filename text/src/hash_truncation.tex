\section{Prefix Truncation}
\label{sec:truncation}

\subsection{The Hash Function}

An alternative locality-preserving hash: $h(x) = \lfloor x / 2^t \rfloor$, keeping the
top $K = L - t$ bits of each key.

Properties of the hash:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Locality-preserving:} truncation preserves order --- if $a < b$, then
    $h(a) \leq h(b)$. Intervals map to intervals.
  \item \textbf{Contiguous phantoms:} the $2^t$ phantom points of each stored key $x$
    form a contiguous interval of length~$2^t$ in the original universe --- all points
    sharing the same $K$-bit prefix. This contrasts with the SODA hash, where phantoms
    are scattered uniformly.
  \item \textbf{Zero storage:} the hash is a bit-shift --- no coefficients to store.
\end{itemize}

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{phantom_comparison}
  \caption{Phantom point distribution: random hash (SODA) scatters phantoms uniformly
    across $U$; truncation concentrates them in a contiguous block adjacent to the key.}

\subsection{ARE Construction With Truncation}

Combining truncation with ERE yields a filter where the contiguous phantom structure
(Figure~\ref{fig:phantom-comparison}) changes the collision analysis:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{SODA hash:} phantoms scattered $\Rightarrow$ collision zone per key is
    $\mathcal{L} \times 2^t$.
  \item \textbf{Truncation:} phantoms contiguous $\Rightarrow$ collision zone per key is
    $\mathcal{L} + 2^t$.
\end{itemize}

The additive (rather than multiplicative) interaction eliminates the $\mathcal{L}$
factor from the filter's space formula:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}llll@{}}
\toprule
Hash & Collision zone & $K$ for FPR $\leq \varepsilon$ & Filter BPK \\
\midrule
Random (SODA)  & $\mathcal{L} \times 2^t$ & $\log_2(n\mathcal{L}/\varepsilon)$ &
  $\log_2(\mathcal{L}/\varepsilon) + O(1)$ \\
Truncation     & $\mathcal{L} + 2^t$      & $\log_2(2n/\varepsilon)$ &
  $\log_2(1/\varepsilon) + O(1)$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Choosing truncation over the SODA hash reduces the resulting filter's BPK by
  $\log_2(\mathcal{L})$. At $\mathcal{L} = 128$, this is a saving of 7~bits per key.}
\label{tab:trunc-vs-soda}
\end{table}

\subsection{The Price of Contiguity}
\label{sec:trunc-failure}

The same property that reduces the filter's space is also the fundamental weakness: all
false positives are concentrated near stored keys. For a stored key~$x$, its phantom
interval $[x - 2^t, x + 2^t]$ is the only region that can produce false positives.

When inter-key gaps are smaller than the phantom size~$2^t$, phantom intervals of
neighboring keys overlap completely and FPR approaches 100\%. This occurs with:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Sequential keys} ($x, x{+}1, x{+}2, \ldots$): every gap is covered by
    phantoms from both sides.
  \item \textbf{Clustered keys}: phantom intervals merge into large continuous zones
    within clusters.
\end{itemize}

Only uniformly spread keys with gaps $\gg 2^t$ achieve the theoretical FPR --- a narrow
use case (Figure~\ref{fig:trunc-uniform}). The SODA hash avoids this entirely: pairwise
independence scatters phantoms uniformly, giving $\Pr[h(x_1) = h(x_2)] \leq 1/r$ for
any distribution, at the cost of $\log_2(\mathcal{L})$ extra bits per key in the
resulting filter.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{trunc_tradeoff_uniform}
  \caption{FPR vs BPK for truncation vs SODA on uniform keys ($n = 2^{18}$,
    $\mathcal{L} = 128$). The SODA curve is shifted right by
    ${\approx}\log_2(128) = 7$~BPK --- matching the theoretical prediction.}
  \label{fig:trunc-uniform}
\end{figure}

\section{Adaptive Filter}
\label{sec:adaptive}

The adaptive filter checks at build time whether hashing is necessary at all.

\subsection{Exact Mode}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Normalize:} subtract $\min(S)$ from all keys, shifting the dataset to
    start at~0.
  \item \textbf{Measure spread:} $M = \lceil \log_2(\max(S) - \min(S)) \rceil$.
  \item \textbf{Decide:}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item $M \leq K$: the entire dataset fits in $K$ bits $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{exact
        mode}. Build ERE directly over normalized keys. No hash, FPR~$= 0$.
      \item $M > K$: data too spread $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{SODA mode}. Apply
        pairwise-independent hash, same filter guarantees as Section~\ref{sec:soda}.
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{When Does Exact Mode Trigger?}

Exact mode requires $\max(S) - \min(S) < 2^K = n\mathcal{L}/\varepsilon$.
Rewriting in terms of density $\rho = n / (\max - \min)$:
\begin{equation}
  \rho > \frac{\varepsilon}{\mathcal{L}}
  \label{eq:exact-threshold}
\end{equation}
or equivalently, the average gap must satisfy
$\bar{g} < \mathcal{L}/\varepsilon$.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}llll@{}}
\toprule
$\mathcal{L}$ & $\varepsilon$ & Max avg gap & Min density \\
\midrule
128 & $10^{-3}$ & 128,000            & $7.8 \times 10^{-6}$ \\
128 & $10^{-6}$ & $1.28 \times 10^8$ & $7.8 \times 10^{-9}$ \\
16  & $10^{-3}$ & 16,000             & $6.25 \times 10^{-5}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Exact mode threshold for typical parameters. The density requirement is
  extremely low --- exact mode fails only when keys are scattered across a universe
  much wider than $n \cdot \mathcal{L} / \varepsilon$.}
\label{tab:exact-threshold}
\end{table}

This becomes especially powerful in combination with hybrid segmentation
(Chapter~\ref{chap:hybrid}): each dense cluster is isolated into its own adaptive filter,
where the local spread is small enough for exact mode, giving FPR~$= 0$ on the dense
parts of the data.
