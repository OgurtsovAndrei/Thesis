\section{Key Distributions in Practice}
\label{sec:distributions}

The theoretical bounds of Chapter~\ref{chap:ere} hold for any key set. In practice,
however, keys in storage systems are not arbitrary --- they follow distributions shaped
by the application domain. Understanding these distributions is essential for evaluating
hash function designs and segmentation strategies in the following chapters.

\subsection{The Core Assumption}
\label{sec:assumption}

We assume that the \emph{query distribution approximately matches the key distribution}:
users query ranges where data exists, not empty regions of the key space. This is
natural for most workloads --- a time-series database receives queries for recent
timestamps (where data was written), not for arbitrary future dates.

This assumption has two consequences:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item FPR in sparse regions of the key space matters less --- queries rarely land there.
  \item Dense regions (where most keys and queries concentrate) dominate the effective FPR
    experienced by the application.
\end{enumerate}

The SODA hash (Section~\ref{sec:soda}) ignores this assumption entirely --- it provides
worst-case guarantees for any distribution. The truncation hash
(Section~\ref{sec:truncation}) implicitly assumes uniformity. The hybrid approaches
(Chapter~\ref{chap:hybrid}) explicitly exploit it by isolating dense regions.

\subsection{Common Industrial Distributions}

Keys in LSM-tree storage systems typically exhibit one or more of the following patterns:

\paragraph{Sequential / near-sequential.}
Auto-incrementing primary keys, monotonic timestamps, log sequence numbers. Keys are
approximately evenly spaced with small gaps. The key space is dense and compact ---
spread is much smaller than the universe.

\paragraph{Clustered.}
Keys concentrate in a few dense regions separated by large gaps. Examples:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{User activity:} a social network stores events keyed by user ID prefix
    + timestamp. Active users produce dense clusters; inactive users leave gaps.
  \item \textbf{Geospatial data:} S2 cell IDs or geohashes cluster around populated
    areas. Rural regions are sparse.
  \item \textbf{Time-partitioned data:} recent partitions are dense (active writes),
    older partitions are sparse (archived).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{High-entropy / uniform-like.}
UUIDs, cryptographic hashes, randomly generated tokens. Keys are spread across the full
key space with no exploitable structure. This is the hardest case for distribution-aware
optimizations and the only case where truncation achieves its theoretical BPK.

\subsection{Synthetic Distributions}

To isolate the effect of specific distribution properties, we also generate synthetic
key sets:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Uniform:} keys drawn uniformly from $[0, 2^{60})$. Average gap
    $\approx 2^{60}/n$. No clustering. Baseline for truncation-friendly data.
  \item \textbf{Clustered:} a mixture of Gaussian clusters with controlled parameters
    (number of clusters, intra-cluster spread, inter-cluster gap). Models the typical
    industrial pattern.
\end{itemize}

Together, the SOSD and synthetic datasets span the spectrum from highly clustered
(Facebook, Books) through moderately structured (Wiki, OSM) to structureless (uniform
random) --- allowing us to evaluate each filter design across the range of distributions
it may encounter in practice.
